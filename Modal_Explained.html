Modal Verbs —
Know When to Use Them

One of the biggest challenges at A2 level is that could, should, must, may and might all look similar. The key is simple: learn them by purpose, not by grammar rules.

⚡ Quick Decision Guide — Ask yourself this question:

Is it a skill or ability?

↓

CAN / CAN'T

Is it advice or a recommendation?

↓

SHOULD / SHOULDN'T

Is it a very strong rule or obligation?

↓

MUST / MUSTN'T

Is it a company rule (not my opinion)?

↓

HAVE TO

Is it one possible idea or option?

↓

COULD

Is it something that is possible / maybe true?

↓

MAY / MIGHT

💪

Modal 1

CAN & CAN'T — Ability

CAN

Use for

Ability — something you know how to do

💪 ▾

Structure

Subject + CAN + verb (base form)

Positive: I can install the software.
Negative: I can't access the server.
Question: Can you use this machine?

✈️ Aviation / Engineering

  • I can read technical drawings.
  • I can operate the forklift.
  • She can use CAD software.

💻 IT / Office

  • I can install the software.
  • He can configure the network.
  • We can solve this remotely.

🍽 Kitchen / Sales

  • I can prepare meals for 200 people.
  • She can speak to the client in English.
  • We can't ship before Friday.
💡 Remember: CAN talks about what you are able to do. If you don't have the skill or it's not possible, use CAN'T.
💡

Modal 2

COULD — Suggestion & Possibility

COULD

Use for

Suggestions · Possibilities · Polite ideas

💡 ▾

Two uses of COULD

Use 1 — Suggestion
We could call the supplier.
👉 "One possible idea."
Use 2 — Possibility
The problem could be the network.
👉 "Maybe. Not certain."

SHOULD

"We should call the supplier."

👉 Good idea. Recommended.

COULD

"We could call the supplier."

👉 One possible option. Softer.

✈️We could delay the maintenance check until Tuesday.
🏭The defect could be in the assembly process.
🚬We could offer the client a discount.
💡 Student tip: COULD is softer and more polite than SHOULD. Use COULD in meetings when you want to suggest an idea without being too direct.
👍

Modal 3

SHOULD & SHOULDN'T — Advice

SHOULD

Use for

Giving advice or a recommendation

👍 ▾

Structure

Subject + SHOULD / SHOULDN'T + verb (base form)

Positive: We should test the component. → Good idea.
Negative: We shouldn't ignore this problem. → Bad idea.

Positive (Good idea)

  • We should check the machine now.
  • You should update the software.
  • The team should communicate more.
  • We should contact the customer.

Negative (Bad idea)

  • We shouldn't ignore the issue.
  • You shouldn't skip the inspection.
  • We shouldn't delay the report.
  • They shouldn't miss the deadline.
🧪 Quick test: Before you speak, ask yourself — "Is this advice?" If yes → use SHOULD. If it's not just advice but a real rule, use MUST or HAVE TO.
🚨

Modal 4

MUST & MUSTN'T — Strong Obligation

MUST

Use for

Strong obligation — very important, no choice

🚨 ▾

Structure

Subject + MUST / MUSTN'T + verb (base form)

Positive: We must finish the report today. → Very important!
Negative: You mustn't touch this equipment. → PROHIBITED. Not allowed.

SHOULD

"You should wear safety glasses."

Good idea. Advice.

MUST

"You must wear safety glasses."

Required. No choice.

MUST (obligation)

  • You must wear safety equipment.
  • We must finish the audit today.
  • All staff must sign in.

MUSTN'T (prohibited)

  • You mustn't smoke in this area.
  • Employees mustn't share passwords.
  • You mustn't enter without a badge.
⚠️ Important: MUSTN'T = NOT ALLOWED. It is much stronger than SHOULDN'T. Use MUSTN'T for safety rules and strict prohibitions.
📋

Modal 5

HAVE TO — External Rules & Requirements

HAVE TO

Use for

Company rules, policies, external requirements

📋 ▾

MUST vs HAVE TO — the most common student question

MUST
The speaker thinks it is important.

"We must improve quality."
→ My opinion. My decision.
HAVE TO
A rule, law, or policy says so.

"We have to wear ID badges."
→ The company says so.

✈️ Aviation

  • We have to complete the pre-flight checklist.
  • Pilots have to report any technical issue.

🏭 Engineering

  • We have to follow the safety protocol.
  • All parts have to be inspected.

🚬 Sales / JTI

  • We have to submit reports monthly.
  • Sales staff have to follow company policy.
💡 Simple rule: MUST = I think it is necessary.  |  HAVE TO = The company / rules say it is necessary.
🎲

Modal 6

MAY & MIGHT — Probability

MAY / MIGHT

Use for

Something is possible but not certain

🎲 ▾

MAY

"The supplier may arrive today."

~50–70% possible. More likely.

MIGHT

"The supplier might be late."

Less certain. Maybe 30–50%.

MAY examples

  • The customer may approve the design.
  • The system may need an update.
  • The delivery may arrive this afternoon.

MIGHT examples

  • The issue might be the software.
  • We might need more time.
  • The flight might be delayed.
💡 For A2 level: MAY and MIGHT mean almost the same thing. Both = "maybe / possible". Don't worry too much about the difference — use either one!
📊

Summary

Master Table — All Modals at a Glance

Modal Meaning Workplace Example
can Ability I can read technical drawings.
can't No ability I can't access the server.
could Suggestion / Possibility We could contact the supplier. The problem could be the network.
should Advice — good idea We should check the system.
shouldn't Advice — bad idea We shouldn't ignore the issue.
must Strong obligation You must wear safety equipment.
mustn't Prohibited You mustn't enter this area.
have to External rule / requirement We have to wear PPE. (Company rule)
may Possible (~50–70%) The delivery may arrive today.
might Maybe (less certain) The delivery might be late.

Practice

Situation Drills — What would you say?

Practice Drill

Read the situation. Study the example answers. Notice which modal is used and why.

📦 Situation 1: The supplier is late.

  • We should call them. SHOULD — advice
  • We could send an email instead. COULD — suggestion
  • They might arrive tomorrow. MIGHT — possibility
  • The delay may affect production. MAY — possibility

⚠️ Situation 2: The machine is dangerous.

  • Employees must wear safety equipment. MUST — strong obligation
  • Workers mustn't touch the machine without training. MUSTN'T — prohibited
  • We have to follow the safety protocol. HAVE TO — company rule

💻 Situation 3: The network is down.

  • We should restart the system. SHOULD — advice
  • We could contact IT support. COULD — suggestion
  • The problem might be the server. MIGHT — possibility
  • I can check the router. CAN — ability

✈️ Situation 4: New safety regulation at the airport.

  • All staff have to complete the training. HAVE TO — regulation
  • You mustn't enter the restricted area without clearance. MUSTN'T — prohibited
  • The flight might be delayed due to weather. MIGHT — uncertainty
