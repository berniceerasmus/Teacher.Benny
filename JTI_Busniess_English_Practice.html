📖 How these words work in English sentences
Nouns used as verbs (conversion)
Many business words can be both a noun and a verb with no change. delegate (noun: a person) → I will delegate this task (verb: to hand over). Same with escalate, benchmark, cc, and upsell. When you see a new word, ask: "Can I use this as a verb too?"
Compound nouns — two words, one meaning
English loves compound nouns: call cycle, market share, shelf share, action points, price tier. The first word describes the second. Stress usually falls on the FIRST word: CALL cycle, SHELF share, ACTION points. Practice stressing the first word.
Prepositions after verbs
These are fixed combinations — you must learn the preposition together with the verb:
follow up WITH someone  |  loop IN someone  |  delegate TO someone  |  escalate TO a manager  |  focus ON a target. There is no grammar rule — memorise these as pairs.
Uncountable nouns (no plural -s)
Some words are always singular and never take -s: feedback ✓   feedbacks ✗  |  compliance ✓   compliances ✗  |  visibility ✓   visibilities ✗  |  coaching ✓   coachings ✗. If unsure, don't add -s.

🔊 Why pronunciation matters in business English
Stress changes meaning
In English, the stressed syllable carries the meaning. Moving the stress can make a word hard to understand even if every letter is correct. Example: REcord (noun: a record) vs reCORD (verb: to record something). Always check which syllable is stressed.
The schwa /ə/ — English's most common sound
Unstressed vowels in English often become a weak "uh" sound called the schwa. Examples: a·GEN·da — the "a" sounds like "uh". com·PLI·ance — both "o" and second "a" are schwas. Don't over-pronounce every vowel — let the unstressed ones be weak.
-tion and -sion endings
Words ending in -tion are always stressed on the syllable BEFORE it and pronounced "shun": ne·go·ti·A·tion, con·CES·sion, SUC·ces·sion. This is a reliable rule — use it for every new word you learn ending in -tion.
Silent letters in business words
debt — the "b" is silent  |  schedule — British English: "SHED-yool"  |  colleague — the "ue" is silent. When in doubt, use the 🔊 button and listen carefully.

🧩 How English sentences are built
Basic word order: Subject → Verb → Object (SVO)
English almost always follows this order. Unlike Turkish, you cannot move the verb to the end.
We / need / to review the targets (S / V / O). I / will delegate / this task. If you're unsure, always put the subject first, then the verb immediately after.
Modal verbs — must, should, can, will, would
Modal verbs come BEFORE the main verb and never take -s or -ed:
must follow (obligation)  |  should focus (advice)  |  can negotiate (ability/possibility)  |  will delegate (future plan)  |  would like to discuss (polite request). Structure: Subject + modal + base verb.
Question word order
In English questions, the auxiliary verb comes BEFORE the subject: Can we negotiate...? not We can negotiate...?  |  Can I follow up...? not I can follow up...?. For yes/no questions use: Can / Could / Would / Do / Did + subject + verb?
Time expressions go at the start or end — not in the middle
✓ This quarter, the team hit their target.  |  ✓ The team hit their target this quarter.
✗ The team this quarter hit their target. — Do not put the time expression between subject and verb.
Polite requests: "I'd like to..." vs "I want to..."
I want to discuss... — grammatically correct but sounds direct/demanding in a business context. I'd like to discuss... — polite, professional, preferred. I'd = I would. Always use I'd like to in meetings, emails, and with customers.
