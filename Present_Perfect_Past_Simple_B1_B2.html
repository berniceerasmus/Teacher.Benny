Present Perfect
vs Past Simple

Understand the key difference between what happened and what it means now — and use both tenses accurately in professional contexts.

B1 · B2 ~45 minutes Business English 8 sections

📐 Grammar at a glance

Present Perfect

have/has + past participle

A past action with a connection to now: result, experience, recent news, or unfinished time.

We have signed the contract. (result = it's done now)

Past Simple

verb + -ed / irregular form

A completed action at a specific, finished time in the past.

We signed the contract yesterday. (specific time = finished)

Key time signals

choose the right tense

Perfect: just, already, yet, recently, ever, never, so far, since, for
Simple: yesterday, last week, in 2022, ago, when, at 9am

Have you sent the report yet? / I sent it this morning.

A regional logistics company recently carried out a full review of its delivery operations. The operations manager, who joined the company three years ago, has introduced several important changes since she took on the role.

Last year, the team upgraded their route-planning software and reduced average delivery times by 18%. They also renegotiated contracts with two major suppliers. Since then, customer satisfaction scores have improved significantly, and the number of late deliveries has fallen.

This quarter, the company has already launched a new driver training programme and has begun collecting data on fuel efficiency. The manager presented the initial results at last month's board meeting. The board has not yet made a final decision on the next investment, but early signs are positive.

1. How long has the operations manager worked at the company?

2. What two things did the team do last year? (Give both.)

3. What has the company already done this quarter?

4. Find one present perfect and one past simple sentence in the text. Write them here.

✓ Suggested answers

  1. Three years.
  2. They upgraded their route-planning software and renegotiated contracts with two major suppliers.
  3. It has launched a new driver training programme and has begun collecting data on fuel efficiency.

Reading

A more complex professional text with analytical questions

B2

When the new operations director took over in early 2022, the company had been struggling with supply chain inefficiencies for nearly two years. By the time she arrived, the previous leadership had attempted several fixes, none of which had produced lasting results.

Over the past eighteen months, however, the situation has changed significantly. The director has restructured the procurement team, renegotiated three key supplier contracts, and introduced a new inventory management system. Delivery lead times, which stood at an average of twelve days when she joined, have since dropped to just four.

Staff retention has also improved. Since the restructure, the company has not lost a single senior specialist — a sharp contrast to the two years that preceded her appointment, during which turnover in the department reached 35%.

The director presented a full progress report at last quarter's investor briefing. Stakeholders responded positively, though several have asked whether the improvements can be sustained as the company prepares to enter two new international markets.

1. What situation had the company been experiencing before the director arrived?

2. Name three specific changes the director has made since joining.

3. Why does the writer use the past perfect ("had been struggling", "had attempted") in the first paragraph? What does it signal?

4. What concern have stakeholders raised? How does the tense used signal that it is still ongoing?

✓ Suggested answers

  1. Supply chain inefficiencies for nearly two years.
  2. Restructured the procurement team; renegotiated supplier contracts; introduced a new inventory management system.
  3. The past perfect signals actions that were completed before another past event (her arrival). It frames the earlier failures as background context, separate from what she has since achieved.
  4. Stakeholders have asked whether improvements can be sustained. The present perfect ("have asked") signals the concern is recent and still relevant — it hasn't been resolved yet.
