How to use this resource

Each section is divided into B1 (competent, clear, functional) and B2 (natural, nuanced, professional). Start with B1 to build confidence, then upgrade to B2.

Every phrase includes its meaning and a real example sentence. Use the Practice section at the end to test yourself.

Key tip: In professional English, disagreement is almost always softened. You rarely say "I disagree" directly. Instead, you acknowledge the other person's point first, then present your alternative view.

Key tip: In English emails, requests are almost always indirect and softened. The more polite and professional the email, the more indirect the language. "Send me the report" → "Would you be able to send me the report at your earliest convenience?"

The STAR method: When answering behavioural questions ("Tell me about a time you…"), use this structure:

S — Situation: Set the context.
T — Task: What was your responsibility?
A — Action: What did you actually do?
R — Result: What was the outcome?

B2 — STAR Phrases
PhrasePartExample

Practice Activities

Task: Rewrite each informal or basic phrase to sound professional. Aim for B1 level — then try to make it even more natural at B2 level.

Rewrite these phrases for a professional email

  1. "I'm writing because I want to ask about the job." → More formal opening?
  2. "Send me the report when you can." → Polite request?
  3. "Sorry for the late reply." → Professional apology?
  4. "Call me if you have questions." → Formal closing offer?
  5. "I talked to the team about it." → Business verb?
  6. "I will do the project by Friday." → Stronger verb?
  7. "Bye / Talk soon" → Professional sign-off?
  8. "Thanks for your help." → More formal expression?
Model answer — example 1:
Basic: "I'm writing because I want to ask about the job."
B1: "I am writing to enquire about the position advertised on your website."
B2: "I am writing to express my interest in the role and to request further information about the selection process."
How it works: Your teacher reads the prompt. You respond using appropriate B1 or B2 language from this resource. Try to use full sentences — not just single words.

Respond to each situation

  1. You want to disagree with a colleague's suggestion in a meeting — do it politely.
  2. Someone says something you didn't understand — ask them to clarify.
  3. You want to add a point to what your colleague just said.
  4. The meeting is going too long — suggest moving on.
  5. You want to raise a concern about a deadline at the end of a meeting.
  6. A client asks if you can lower your price significantly — respond professionally.
  7. Your manager asks you to describe your biggest achievement.
  8. You need to follow up on a task someone has not completed — do it professionally.
  9. You are on a video call and someone's audio is breaking up — manage it.
  10. You meet a new colleague at a company event — start a conversation.

Part 4 — Speaking production (2–3 sentences each)

  1. You're in an interview. The interviewer says: "Tell me about yourself." — Respond.
  2. Describe a time you solved a difficult problem at work. Use the STAR method.
  3. You receive a low offer in a negotiation — respond professionally.
  4. Write an opening line and closing line for a professional email to a client you have never met.
  5. Describe your biggest professional achievement using B2 language.

Homework

This Week's Business English Challenge

Choose ONE of the following tasks and complete it in full. Bring it to your next lesson.

Option A: Write a professional email to a client you have never met, introducing yourself and your company. Use phrases from the Emails section. Minimum 8 sentences.

Option B: Prepare a 2-minute self-introduction as if you are at a job interview. Cover: your background, your strengths, one achievement, and why you want the role. Use the STAR method for the achievement.

Rule: Do not use "I think," "very," or "good" more than once in the entire text. Upgrade every instance.
