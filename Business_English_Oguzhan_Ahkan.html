Grammar for Sales Professionals

Practical grammar points with real examples from sales, negotiation and client communication.

1. Conditional Sentences (If / Would) — Negotiating

1. Conditional Sentences (If / Would) — Negotiating

Use the second conditional (If + past, would + verb) to make polite proposals and hypothetical offers in sales conversations.

✓ If you increased the volume to 10 MT, we would offer a 5% discount.

✓ If the payment terms were net 60, we could confirm the order today.

✗ If you will increase the volume, we would offer a discount.

2. Modal Verbs for Polite Requests & Offers

2. Modal Verbs for Polite Requests & Offers

In professional sales English, use modal verbs to sound polite and confident rather than too direct.

✓ Could you share your volume forecast for Q3? (polite request)

✓ We would be happy to arrange a sample delivery. (polite offer)

✓ Shall I send the technical data sheet? (polite suggestion)

✗ Send me your forecast. / We send you a sample.

3. Present Perfect — Updates & Progress

3. Present Perfect — Updates & Progress

Use the Present Perfect (have/has + past participle) to describe recent events that are relevant now — very common in follow-up emails and calls.

✓ I have sent you the revised quotation. Please check your inbox.

✓ We have already dispatched the sample to your factory.

✗ I sent you the revised quotation yesterday at 3pm. (use Past Simple only for a specific time)

4. Passive Voice — Describing Processes & Products

4. Passive Voice — Describing Processes & Products

The passive voice (is/are/was + past participle) is common when talking about production processes or what has been done, without focusing on who did it.

✓ This couverture is sourced from certified West African farms.

✓ The beans are fermented for 5–7 days before drying.

✓ The contract has been signed and the order has been confirmed.

✗ We source this couverture from certified farms. (less formal in product descriptions)

5. Softening Negative News

5. Softening Negative News

In sales, you often need to say "no" or deliver bad news without damaging the relationship. Use "I'm afraid…", "Unfortunately…", or "I regret to inform you…"

✓ I'm afraid that price is outside our current budget.

✓ Unfortunately, we are unable to meet that delivery date.

✓ I regret to inform you that the product has been discontinued.

✗ No, we can't do that. / That's impossible.

6. Talking About Numbers & Quantities

6. Talking About Numbers & Quantities

Use the correct prepositions and structures when discussing volumes, percentages and targets in sales conversations.

✓ We are looking at approximately 20 metric tonnes per month.

✓ Sales increased by 12% compared to the same quarter last year.

✓ The discount applies from 5 MT upwards.

✗ We look at 20 metric tonnes. / Sales increased with 12%.

Scenario 1: Following Up After a Tasting Session
📞

Scenario 1: Following Up After a Tasting Session

You call a client after sending them chocolate samples last week.

You

Good morning, Mehmet Bey. This is Oğuzhan Akan calling from Barry Callebaut. I hope you had a chance to try the samples we sent last week?

Client

Yes, actually — we did. The team liked the 54% dark couverture very much, but the milk chocolate was a bit too sweet for our recipe.

You

Thank you for the feedback — that's very useful. For your milk chocolate application, I would actually recommend our Callebaut 823 instead, which has a lower sugar content and a more balanced flavour profile. I can arrange a new sample immediately.

Client

That sounds interesting. What's the lead time if we decide to order?

You

For standard orders, our lead time is five working days from confirmed purchase order. If you'd like to trial a small quantity first, we can arrange 25 kg at no additional cost. Shall I send you a formal quotation?

Client

Yes, please send it over. We'll review and get back to you this week.

Tip Notice the structure: acknowledge feedback → offer an alternative → give a clear next step. "Shall I…?" is a very professional way to propose action without being pushy.

Scenario 3: Handling a Price Objection
🤝

Scenario 3: Handling a Price Objection

A client says your price is too high compared to a competitor.

Client

Oğuzhan Bey, to be honest, we received a lower offer from another supplier. Their price is 8% cheaper than yours.

You

I appreciate you sharing that with me — it's important to compare all options. May I ask if the competing product is also Rainforest Alliance certified and comes with a full Certificate of Analysis? These are often points where quality differs.

Client

I'm not sure about the certification, actually.

You

That's worth checking. Our couverture is fully certified and comes with Barry Callebaut's technical support, which includes recipe development and on-site troubleshooting. When you factor in these added values, the total cost is often very competitive. That said, if you could share your target price, I'll see what I can do.

Client

Alright. Our target is around €X. Can you match that?

You

That's close to our volume discount threshold. If you can commit to 2,000 kg per order, I believe we can make that work. Let me confirm internally and come back to you by tomorrow.

Tip When handling price objections: don't drop the price immediately. Instead, highlight value → ask questions → link the discount to higher commitment. "That's worth checking" and "when you factor in…" are very useful phrases.
Scenario 4: Handling a Delivery Complaint
🚨

Scenario 4: Handling a Delivery Complaint

A client calls to say their order arrived late and one pallet was damaged.

Client

Oğuzhan Bey, I have to say we're quite unhappy. The order arrived two days late and one pallet of couverture was clearly damaged during transit.

You

I completely understand your frustration, and I sincerely apologise for both the delay and the damage. This is not the level of service we aim to provide. Could you please send me photos of the damaged pallet along with the batch number? I will raise this with our logistics team immediately.

Client

Of course. We need a replacement quickly — we have a production run on Friday.

You

Understood — Friday is our priority. I will arrange an urgent replacement shipment today and ensure it reaches you by Thursday at the latest. I will send you the tracking details as soon as the order is dispatched.

Client

Thank you, Oğuzhan Bey. Please make sure it doesn't happen again.

You

Absolutely — I'll personally follow up until this is resolved. We will also conduct an internal review to prevent this from happening in the future. I appreciate your patience.

Tip Use this four-step structure for complaints: apologise sincerely → gather information → give a clear solution with a deadline → reassure. Never argue about who is at fault on the first call.
