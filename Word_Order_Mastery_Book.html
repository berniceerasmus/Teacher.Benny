Abbreviation Guide
What do the letters mean?

Every sentence formula in this workbook uses abbreviations. Learn what each letter stands for before you begin — these are the building blocks of every sentence you will ever construct in English.

S
Subject
WHO or WHAT performs the action. Usually a noun, pronoun, or noun phrase.
V
Verb
The action or state. Every sentence must have a verb — one word or a verb phrase.
O
Object
WHAT receives the action. Comes after the verb in most English sentences.
DO
Direct Object
The thing directly affected by the verb. "She wrote a letter." → letter = DO.
IO
Indirect Object
The person who receives the DO. "She gave him a letter." → him = IO.
B
Be (linking verb)
am / is / are / was / were — used to describe or identify the subject.
Adj
Adjective
A describing word. Comes before a noun or after a linking verb.
Adv
Adverb
Modifies a verb, adjective, or adverb. Describes HOW. Often ends in -ly.
P
Prepositional Phrase
Preposition + noun. Tells WHERE, WHEN, or HOW. e.g. "at school", "on Monday".
C
Complement
Completes the meaning of the subject or object. Can be a noun, adjective, or phrase.
SC
Subject Complement
Describes the subject after a linking verb. "She seems tired." → tired = SC.
OC
Object Complement
Describes the object. "They elected her president." → president = OC.
T
Time Expression
Tells WHEN. Usually placed at the end of a sentence in English.
Pl
Place Expression
Tells WHERE. Placed before Time in longer sentences.
Freq
Frequency Adverb
Tells HOW OFTEN. Before main verb, but after "be". always / usually / often / rarely / never.
Neg
Negative Particle
"not" or contractions: don't, doesn't, didn't. Always placed after an auxiliary verb.

Level A1 — Beginner
A1 — Beginner

Your First Sentences

Subject · Verb · Object · Simple descriptions · Location

Every English sentence has at least two parts: something that does the action (the Subject) and the action itself (the Verb). This is the most important rule in English grammar, and it never changes. Whether you are speaking, writing, or texting — the Subject always comes first, and the Verb always comes second.

Think of English word order like a train. The Subject is the engine at the front. The Verb is the second carriage. Everything else comes after. Unlike many other languages, English does not allow you to put the verb before the subject in a normal statement. The position of a word tells you its job in the sentence.

Formula 1 — S + BE + Adjective

The simplest way to describe something in English is to use the verb be (am, is, are) followed by an adjective. The adjective always comes after the verb — never before it in a sentence. This structure is used constantly: to describe feelings, physical states, weather, and personality.

Formula 1
S + BE + Adjective
Subject + am / is / are / was / were + describing word
Examples
She is happy.
S = She · B = is · Adj = happy
They are tired.
S = They · B = are · Adj = tired
The weather is cold.
S = The weather · B = is · Adj = cold
I am ready.
S = I · B = am · Adj = ready
Common Mistakes
Happy she is.
She is happy. (adjective comes after "be", not before the subject)
She happy is.
She is happy. (verb "be" must come second, before the adjective)
She is very happy. ✓ / She very is happy. ✗
"Very" goes before the adjective: She is very happy.
Formula 2 — S + V (Intransitive)

Some verbs do not need an object — the action is complete on its own. These are called intransitive verbs. Common examples: run, sleep, cry, laugh, arrive, wait, sit, stand. A sentence with an intransitive verb is complete with just a Subject and a Verb. You do not need to add anything else.

Formula 2
S + V
Subject + action verb — no object needed or possible
Examples
She sleeps.
S = She · V = sleeps
The baby cried.
S = The baby · V = cried
We waited.
S = We · V = waited
He laughed.
S = He · V = laughed
Teacher Benny's Tip: Some verbs can be both transitive and intransitive depending on context. "She reads" (S+V) and "She reads books" (S+V+O) are both correct — the verb "read" works both ways.
Formula 3 — S + V + Object

Most English verbs need an object — they describe an action that is done to something or someone. The object always comes after the verb. Never before it. This S + V + O pattern is the backbone of English grammar. Master this and you can build thousands of correct sentences. Common transitive verbs: eat, drink, read, write, watch, like, love, have, want, need.

Formula 3
S + V + O
Subject + action verb + thing or person receiving the action
Examples
She drinks coffee.
S = She · V = drinks · O = coffee
He reads books.
S = He · V = reads · O = books
They love music.
S = They · V = love · O = music
I have a dog.
S = I · V = have · O = a dog
Common Mistakes
Coffee she drinks.
She drinks coffee. (object never comes before subject in statements)
Drinks she coffee?
Does she drink coffee? (questions need "does" — see A2)
She drink coffee.
She drinks coffee. (third person singular needs -s)
Formula 4 — S + BE + Prepositional Phrase

To say where something is, use the verb be followed by a prepositional phrase. A prepositional phrase begins with a preposition (in, on, at, under, next to, between, behind, in front of) followed by a noun. This structure is used constantly to describe locations. The key rule: the preposition always comes before its noun.

Formula 4
S + BE + Preposition + Noun
Describes location. Preposition always comes directly before its noun phrase.
Examples
The book is on the table.
P = on the table
She is at school.
P = at school
They are in the garden.
P = in the garden
The cat is under the chair.
P = under the chair
Common Mistakes
The book on the table is.
The book is on the table. (verb "be" must come before the phrase)
She is school.
She is at school. (preposition "at" is required)
Teacher Benny's Tip: "In", "on" and "at" are the three most common prepositions of place. Use "in" for enclosed spaces (in the room, in London), "on" for surfaces (on the table, on the floor), and "at" for specific points (at school, at the bus stop).
Level A2 — Elementary
A2 — Elementary

Expanding Your Sentences

Frequency adverbs · Time & Place · Yes/No questions · Negatives

At A2, you take the basic S + V + O structure and start adding more information. You learn to say how often (frequency adverbs), when (time expressions), and where (place expressions). You also learn two critical new sentence types: questions and negatives.

The most important new rule at this level is the placement of adverbs. English has very strict rules about where adverbs go — and many learners get this wrong for years. Master it now and you will sound much more natural immediately.

Formula 5 — Frequency Adverbs (before main verb)

Frequency adverbs tell us how often something happens: always, usually, often, sometimes, rarely, seldom, never. In English, these adverbs slot in between the subject and the main verb. This is a fixed position — they do not go at the beginning or end of the sentence in normal usage. The order from most to least frequent is: always → usually → often → sometimes → rarely → never.

Formula 5
S + Freq + V + O
Frequency adverb sits between Subject and main Verb — never at the start or end
Examples
She always drinks coffee.
Freq = always · before "drinks"
I usually walk to work.
Freq = usually · before "walk"
They rarely eat meat.
Freq = rarely · before "eat"
He never watches TV.
Freq = never · before "watches"
Common Mistakes
Always she drinks coffee.
She always drinks coffee. (adverb cannot start the sentence here)
She drinks always coffee.
She always drinks coffee. (adverb cannot go between verb and object)
She drinks coffee always.
She always drinks coffee. (adverb does not go at the end)
Formula 6 — Frequency Adverbs with BE (after the verb)

There is one important exception to Formula 5. When the verb is be (am, is, are, was, were), the frequency adverb comes after the verb, not before it. This surprises many learners who have just memorised Formula 5 — but the rule is clear: frequency adverbs follow "be" and precede all other verbs.

Formula 6
S + BE + Freq + Adjective / Noun
With the verb "be", the frequency adverb follows the verb
Examples
She is always late.
B = is · Freq = always · after "is"
He is never rude.
B = is · Freq = never · after "is"
We are usually ready.
B = are · Freq = usually · after "are"
They are sometimes late.
B = are · Freq = sometimes · after "are"
Teacher Benny's Tip: Easy rule to remember — with all normal verbs the adverb goes before; with the verb BE the adverb goes after. Compare: "She always runs" vs "She is always tired."
Formula 7 — Place and Time (S + V + O + Place + Time)

When you add information about where and when something happens, English has a specific order. Place comes before Time. Both go at the end of the sentence, after the object. The golden rule to memorise is MPT: Manner → Place → Time. You do not need to use all three every time, but whenever you do, this is the order they must appear in.

Formula 7
S + V + O + Place + Time
Place expression comes before Time expression — both go at the end of the sentence
Examples
She works in London every day.
Pl = in London · T = every day
He plays football at the park on Sundays.
Pl = at the park · T = on Sundays
They met at the café yesterday.
Pl = at the café · T = yesterday
I study at home every evening.
Pl = at home · T = every evening
Common Mistakes
She works every day in London.
She works in London every day. (Place before Time)
Every day she works in London. (unusual emphasis)
She works in London every day. (standard order)
Formula 8 — Yes/No Questions (Aux + S + V + O?)

To form a question in the present simple or past simple, English uses an auxiliary verb (do, does, did) that moves to the front of the sentence, before the subject. The main verb returns to its base form — it loses its -s or -ed ending. This is called subject-auxiliary inversion and it is one of the most important patterns in English. Remember: "do" is used with I/you/we/they; "does" is used with he/she/it; "did" is used for all subjects in the past.

Formula 8
Aux + S + V + O ?
Do / Does / Did moves to the front. Main verb returns to base form (no -s, no -ed).
Examples — statement vs question
She drinks coffee. → Does she drink coffee?
does + she + drink (base form)
They live here. → Do they live here?
do + they + live (base form)
He called you. → Did he call you?
did + he + call (base form)
She arrived late. → Did she arrive late?
did + she + arrive (base form)
Common Mistakes
Does she drinks coffee?
Does she drink coffee? (no -s after "does")
Did she arrived late?
Did she arrive late? (base form after "did")
She does drink coffee? (spoken only)
Does she drink coffee? (inversion required in written form)
Formula 9 — Negatives (S + Aux + not + V + O)

To make a negative sentence, you add not after the auxiliary verb (do not, does not, did not). In natural speech these are almost always contracted: don't, doesn't, didn't. The main verb stays in its base form. One critical rule in standard English: you only use one negative word per clause. Never combine "don't" with "never" or "nothing" — this creates a double negative, which is not used in standard grammar.

Formula 9
S + Aux + not + V + O
Do/Does/Did + not goes between Subject and main Verb. Contractions are very common.
Examples
She does not drink coffee.
= She doesn't drink coffee.
They do not live here.
= They don't live here.
He did not call.
= He didn't call.
I do not understand.
= I don't understand.
Common Mistakes
She doesn't likes coffee.
She doesn't like coffee. (base form after "doesn't")
She not drink coffee.
She does not drink coffee. (auxiliary verb required)
She don't never drink coffee.
She doesn't ever drink coffee. / She never drinks coffee.
Level B1 — Intermediate
B1 — Intermediate

Connecting & Complexifying

Double objects · Compound sentences · Complex clauses · Reported speech

At B1, you move from simple sentences to more sophisticated structures. You learn how to give and send things using the double object construction, and you discover how to join ideas together using coordinating and subordinating conjunctions. These skills allow you to express cause and effect, contrast, and sequence — essential for both speaking and writing.

You also begin working with reported speech, which requires a specific change to word order inside the clause. This level is where English grammar starts to feel like a system you can use creatively, rather than rules to memorise one by one.

Formula 10 — Double Object (S + V + IO + DO)

Some verbs can take two objects: a Direct Object (the thing given or sent) and an Indirect Object (the person who receives it). There are two patterns — and they mean exactly the same thing. When the indirect object comes first, no preposition is needed. When the direct object comes first, you need "to" or "for". Common verbs with this pattern: give, send, show, teach, tell, bring, offer, lend, pay, write, make, buy, cook, get.

Formula 10a — IO first (no preposition)
S + V + IO + DO
Person (IO) before thing (DO) — no preposition needed
Formula 10b — DO first (preposition required)
S + V + DO + to / for + IO
Thing (DO) before person (IO) — preposition "to" or "for" required
Examples — both patterns mean the same thing
She gave him a book.
IO = him · DO = a book
She gave a book to him.
DO = a book · to + IO
He sent her a message.
IO = her · DO = a message
He sent a message to her.
DO = a message · to + IO
Common Mistakes
She gave to him a book.
She gave him a book. (no "to" when IO comes first)
She told to me the news.
She told me the news. ("tell" never uses "to" + IO first)
She bought to him flowers.
She bought flowers for him. ("buy" uses "for", not "to")
Teacher Benny's Tip: Verbs like give, send, show, teach, tell, lend, offer use "to" + IO. Verbs like buy, make, cook, get, find use "for" + IO. There is no rule — you need to learn each verb's preposition.
Formula 11 — Compound Sentences (coordinating conjunctions)

A compound sentence joins two independent clauses (two complete sentences) using a coordinating conjunction. The most common ones are: and (addition), but (contrast), so (result), or (alternative), yet (unexpected contrast), for (reason — formal). A comma usually comes before the conjunction when joining two full clauses. Each clause keeps its own S + V structure — you are not removing anything.

Formula 11
S + V + O , + and / but / so / or + S + V + O
Comma before the conjunction. Each side is a complete sentence.
Examples
It rained, so we stayed inside.
cause → result
She was tired, but she kept working.
contrast
You can call me or send a message.
alternative
He studied hard and he passed the exam.
addition
Formula 12 — Complex Sentences (subordinating conjunctions)

A complex sentence has a main clause and a subordinate (dependent) clause. The subordinate clause begins with a subordinating conjunction such as: because, although, when, if, before, after, while, unless, until, since, as soon as. The subordinate clause can come before or after the main clause. When it comes first, you add a comma between the two clauses. When it comes second, no comma is needed.

Formula 12a — subordinate clause second
S + V + O + because / although / when + S + V
No comma when the subordinate clause comes at the end
Formula 12b — subordinate clause first
Because / Although / When + S + V , + S + V + O
Comma required when the subordinate clause comes first
Examples
She was tired because she worked all day.
Sub clause at end — no comma
Because she worked all day, she was tired.
Sub clause first — comma needed
Although it was cold, she wore a t-shirt.
Contrast — comma after sub clause
If you leave now, you will catch the train.
Condition — comma after if-clause
Common Mistakes
Although it was cold, but she went out.
Although it was cold, she went out. (never use "but" after "although")
Because she was tired. (fragment)
She stayed home because she was tired. (subordinate clause needs a main clause)
Formula 13 — Reported Speech

When you report what someone said, the word order inside the reported clause changes. For reported questions, the word order reverts to normal statement order — no inversion, no "do/does/did". For reported statements, the tense usually shifts back one step in time (present → past, past → past perfect). This is called "backshift". Reported speech is introduced by verbs like say, tell, ask, explain, claim, mention, wonder.

Formula 13a — Reported Statement
S + said (that) + S + V + O
Statement word order in the reported clause. "That" is optional.
Formula 13b — Reported Question
S + asked + if/whether/wh-word + S + V + O
Statement word order — NO inversion, no do/does/did in the reported clause
Examples
"I am tired." → She said she was tired.
am → was (backshift)
"I live here." → He said he lived there.
live → lived
"Do you want tea?" → She asked if I wanted tea.
question → if + statement order
"Where do you work?" → He asked where I worked.
NO inversion in reported question
Common Mistakes
He asked where did I live.
He asked where I lived. (no inversion in reported questions)
She said that she is tired.
She said that she was tired. (backshift: is → was)
Teacher Benny's Tip: Reported questions are one of the most common B1 errors. Always ask yourself: is this a question or a statement now? After "asked", it becomes a statement — use statement word order. "He asked where I worked" — not "where did I work".

Emphasis & Inversion

Negative fronting · Cleft sentences · Pseudo-cleft · Passive voice

At B2, you learn how to change the focus of a sentence by moving certain elements to the front. In English, the beginning of a sentence carries the most emphasis — so by placing unusual elements first, you draw the reader's or listener's attention to specific information. This is a mark of sophisticated, natural-sounding English.

You also master the passive voice and two powerful emphasis structures: cleft sentences and negative inversion. These appear regularly in formal writing, academic English, journalism, and strong spoken argument.

Formula 14 — Negative Fronting + Inversion

When a negative or restrictive adverb — never, rarely, hardly, seldom, only then, not only, no sooner, little, scarcely — is placed at the front of a sentence for emphasis, the subject and auxiliary verb must invert (swap positions). This is called negative inversion. It is mandatory — you cannot front these adverbs without inverting. This structure is common in formal and academic writing and in literary prose.

Formula 14
Negative Adverb + Aux + S + V + O
After fronting never / rarely / hardly / seldom, subject and auxiliary must swap
Examples — normal vs emphatic
Normal: I have never seen this.
Standard word order
Emphatic: Never have I seen this.
Fronted — Aux (have) + S (I) inverted
Normal: She rarely complains.
Standard word order
Emphatic: Rarely does she complain.
Fronted — does + she inverted
Common Mistakes
Only then she understood.
Only then did she understand. (inversion required after "only then")
Rarely she complains.
Rarely does she complain. (inversion is mandatory, not optional)
No sooner she arrived than it rained.
No sooner had she arrived than it rained.
Formula 15 — It-Cleft Sentences

A cleft sentence splits a simple sentence into two clauses to highlight one specific piece of information. The most common type begins with "It was/is…" and ends with "…that/who + rest of sentence." Use "who" when focusing on a person; use "that" for everything else — things, places, times, reasons. Cleft sentences are very common in spoken English (to correct misunderstandings) and in written English (to emphasise key information).

Formula 15 — It-cleft
It + BE + [focus element] + that / who + S + V
Focus element (person, thing, time, place) placed between "It is/was" and "that/who"
Examples — normal vs cleft
Tom broke the window. → It was Tom who broke the window.
Focus: the person (Tom)
She left at midnight. → It was at midnight that she left.
Focus: the time (midnight)
They met in Paris. → It was in Paris that they met.
Focus: the place (Paris)
The noise woke me. → It was the noise that woke me.
Focus: the cause (the noise)
Common Mistakes
It was in Paris where they met.
It was in Paris that they met. (always "that" — not "where", "when" or "which")
It was Tom that broke the window. ✓ / It was Tom who broke it. ✓
Both are correct — "who" is preferred for people, "that" is acceptable too.
Formula 16 — Pseudo-Cleft (What-cleft)

The pseudo-cleft puts emphasis on the most important or new information by placing it at the end of the sentence after "is/was." It begins with a "What" clause (which acts as the subject of the sentence) and places the focused element after the linking verb. This structure is very common in natural spoken English — you will hear it constantly in conversations, interviews, and presentations.

Formula 16
What + S + V + BE + [focus element]
"What I need is…" — the most important information comes last, after "is/was"
Examples
What I need is more time.
Focus = more time
What surprised me was her reaction.
Focus = her reaction
What he did was ignore my advice.
Focus = the action
What we want is a clear answer.
Focus = a clear answer
Formula 17 — Passive Voice

The passive voice changes the focus of the sentence from the person doing the action (the agent) to the person or thing receiving it. It is formed with the verb be + past participle. The agent can be included with "by" or omitted entirely — and often it is omitted when the agent is unknown, unimportant, or obvious. Passive voice is extremely common in academic writing, news reports, and formal documents.

Formula 17
S + BE + past participle (+ by + Agent)
The original object becomes the new subject. Agent with "by" is optional.
Examples — active vs passive
Active: The committee rejected his proposal.
Agent = the committee
Passive: His proposal was rejected (by the committee).
New focus = his proposal
Active: Someone has stolen my bag.
Vague / unknown agent
Passive: My bag has been stolen.
Agent omitted — better here
Teacher Benny's Tip: Use the passive when the action is more important than the person who did it, when the agent is unknown, or when you want to sound more formal or objective. Never use the passive just to sound clever — use it with purpose.
Level C1 — Advanced
C1 — Advanced

Mastery Structures

Participial phrases · Formal conditionals · Not only … but also · Stylistic control

At C1, the structures you learn are less about new grammar rules and more about achieving precision, elegance, and rhetorical power. The word order choices at this level are often stylistic — they allow you to control rhythm, emphasis, formality, and information flow in a way that marks you as an educated, fluent user of English.

These structures appear regularly in high-quality journalism, academic papers, formal speeches, business writing, and literary prose. Mastering them will significantly raise the level and impact of your written English. Use them sparingly — they are powerful precisely because they are unusual.

Formula 18 — Fronted Participial Phrases

A participial phrase is a verb phrase that acts as a modifier — it tells us more about the subject. When placed at the front of a sentence, it must refer to the same subject as the main clause. If it does not, it creates a dangling participle — a serious grammatical error. Fronted participial phrases compress information elegantly and are widely used in formal and literary writing. There are two types: past participial (V-ed) and present participial (V-ing).

Formula 18a — Past participle (V-ed) — often expresses a passive or completed state
V-ed + phrase , + S + V + O
The subject of the main clause must be the person/thing described by the phrase
Formula 18b — Present participle (V-ing) — expresses reason, manner, or simultaneous action
V-ing + phrase , + S + V + O
Use "Having + V-ed" when the participial action was completed before the main action
Examples
Exhausted by the journey, she fell asleep immediately.
Past participial — passive meaning
Realising her mistake, she apologised at once.
Present participial — simultaneous reason
Defeated in the election, he retired from politics.
Compressed passive meaning
Having finished her work, she left the office.
Perfect participial — first action completed
Common Mistakes — Dangling Participles
Walking down the street, the rain started.
Walking down the street, she was caught in the rain. (subject must match)
Having eaten dinner, the dishes were washed.
Having eaten dinner, she washed the dishes. (the dishes did not eat dinner)
Formula 19 — Formal Conditional Inversion

In formal English, conditional sentences (if-clauses) can be written without "if" by inverting the subject and auxiliary verb. This construction is common in legal, academic, and business writing. There are three main patterns corresponding to the three conditional types. The result is a more formal, authoritative tone — often used in contracts, official correspondence, and academic argument.

Formula 19a — 1st Conditional formal (Should)
Should + S + V , + S + will / can + V
"Should you need help" = "If you need help" — real possibility
Formula 19b — 2nd Conditional formal (Were … to)
Were + S + to + V , + S + would + V
"Were she to leave" = "If she left" — hypothetical / unlikely
Formula 19c — 3rd Conditional formal (Had)
Had + S + V-ed , + S + would have + V-ed
"Had I known" = "If I had known" — impossible / past unreal
Examples
Should you require assistance, please contact us.
Formal 1st conditional
Were she to resign, the company would struggle.
Formal 2nd conditional
Had I known earlier, I would have acted differently.
Formal 3rd conditional
Were it not for her support, we would have failed.
"Were it not for" = "If it were not for"
Common Mistakes
Should you will need help, call us.
Should you need help, call us. ("should" is followed by the base form)
Were she leave, we would follow.
Were she to leave, we would follow. ("to" is required in "were … to" conditionals)
Formula 20 — Not Only … But Also

This is a powerful correlative conjunction structure used to add information emphatically — it signals that something is more surprising or impressive than expected. When "Not only" appears at the beginning of a clause (fronted), it triggers subject-auxiliary inversion in the first clause — exactly like the negative adverbs at B2. When "not only" appears mid-sentence (not fronted), no inversion occurs. This structure is very common in formal speeches, persuasive writing, and academic essays.

Formula 20a — Fronted (inversion required in first clause)
Not only + Aux + S + V , but + S + also + V
Inversion in the first clause only. The second clause uses normal word order.
Formula 20b — Mid-sentence (no inversion)
S + not only + V + O , but also + V + O
No inversion when "not only" appears after the subject
Examples
Not only did he win the race, but he also set a new record.
Fronted — inversion (did he)
Not only is she talented, but she is also hardworking.
Fronted — inversion (is she)
She not only speaks French but also reads Latin.
Mid-sentence — no inversion
Not only had they arrived late, but they had also forgotten the documents.
Past perfect — had they inverted
Common Mistakes
Not only she speaks French but also German.
Not only does she speak French, but she also speaks German.
Not only did he win, but also set a record.
Not only did he win, but he also set a record. (second clause needs its own subject)
Teacher Benny's Tip: At C1, the key skill is not just knowing the rule but knowing when to use these structures for effect. Use negative inversion, cleft sentences, and participial phrases sparingly. They are powerful precisely because they are unusual. In every ten sentences, perhaps one or two should use these advanced patterns — any more and the effect is lost.
