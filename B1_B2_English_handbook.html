How to Use This Handbook

This handbook is designed for adult B1 and B2 learners. It explains important grammar in simple language and shows how English is used in real life. Each unit includes explanations, examples, common mistakes, a speaking tip, a mini vocabulary box, and short practice tasks.

This is a handbook, not a workbook. The exercises are short and simple, so learners can check understanding without long practice pages. Answers are not included.

Part 1 — Building Confidence

Unit 1 — Sentence Structure & Word Order

What is this used for?

In English, word order is very important because it helps people understand you clearly. Most English sentences follow a fixed structure. Changing the order can make the sentence sound unnatural or incorrect.

Basic pattern: Subject + Verb + Object

Key Rules

  • Put the subject before the verb.
  • Time expressions usually go at the beginning or end.
  • Adverbs like always, often, and never usually go before the main verb.
  • With the verb be, adverbs usually go after it.

Real-Life Examples

✓ I drink coffee every morning.
✓ She works in a hospital.
✓ He is always late.

UK: I have lunch at half past one.
US: I have lunch at one-thirty.

Common Mistakes

✗ Drink I coffee every morning.
✓ I drink coffee every morning.

Speaking Tip

Start simple. Say: “I work.” Then add more: “I work in London.” Then add time: “I work in London during the week.”

Mini Vocabulary: wake up, go to work, eat breakfast, check emails, go to bed
Quick Practice
  1. Put the words in order: always / I / check / my phone / in the morning
  2. Choose: She every day works / She works every day
  3. Fix: He goes always to bed late.

Unit 2 — Present Simple vs Present Continuous

What is this used for?

We use both tenses to talk about the present, but they show different meanings. Present simple is for routines, facts, and regular situations. Present continuous is for actions happening now, temporary situations, and changes.

Key Rules

  • Present simple: subject + base verb. Add -s for he, she, it.
  • Present continuous: subject + am/is/are + verb-ing.
  • Use present simple with words like always, usually, often, and every day.
  • Use present continuous with now, right now, at the moment, and today.

Real-Life Examples

✓ I work in an office.
✓ I’m working from home today.
✓ She studies English every week.
✓ She is studying right now.

UK: I’m having lunch.
US: I’m eating lunch.

Common Mistakes

✗ I am work now.
✓ I am working now.

Speaking Tip

Ask yourself: Is this always true, or only happening now? Always true = present simple. Happening now = present continuous.

Mini Vocabulary: every day, right now, at the moment, usually, today
Quick Practice
  1. She (works / is working) right now.
  2. I usually ______ coffee in the morning.
  3. Fix: He is play football now.

Unit 3 — Past Simple vs Past Continuous

What is this used for?

We use past simple for completed actions in the past. We use past continuous for actions that were happening over time. These two tenses are often used together when telling stories.

Key Rules

  • Past simple: subject + past verb.
  • Past continuous: subject + was/were + verb-ing.
  • Use past continuous for the background situation.
  • Use past simple for the main event or interruption.

Real-Life Examples

✓ I watched a movie last night.
✓ I was watching TV when she called.
✓ They were walking when it started to rain.

UK: I was queuing when the bus arrived.
US: I was standing in line when the bus arrived.

Common Mistakes

✗ I was watch TV.
✓ I was watching TV.

Speaking Tip

Use past continuous to set the scene and past simple for the important action.

Mini Vocabulary: walk, drive, watch, arrive, call, start
Quick Practice
  1. I (watched / was watching) TV when he called.
  2. They ______ in the park when it started to rain.
  3. Fix: He was play football when I saw him.

Unit 4 — Present Perfect: Intro & Real Use

What is this used for?

We use the present perfect to connect the past to the present. The exact time is not usually important. The result, experience, or connection to now is more important.

Key Rules

  • Structure: subject + have/has + past participle.
  • Use it for life experiences.
  • Use it for recent actions with a present result.
  • Use it with unfinished time periods like today, this week, and this year.

Real-Life Examples

✓ I have visited Paris.
✓ She has already left.
✓ I have lost my keys.

UK: Have you eaten yet?
US: Did you eat yet? / Have you eaten yet?

Common Mistakes

✗ I have seen him yesterday.
✓ I saw him yesterday.

Speaking Tip

If the exact time is important, use past simple. If the experience or result is important, use present perfect.

Mini Vocabulary: been, gone, seen, done, made, taken
Quick Practice
  1. I (have finished / finished) my work just now.
  2. She has ______ to the shop.
  3. Fix: He have just arrived.

Unit 5 — Talking About the Future

What is this used for?

English has several ways to talk about the future. We choose the form based on meaning. Will, going to, and present continuous are the most common future forms.

Key Rules

  • Will: decisions made now, promises, offers, and opinions about the future.
  • Going to: plans decided before now and predictions with evidence.
  • Present continuous: arranged future plans.

Real-Life Examples

✓ I’ll call you later.
✓ I’m going to start a new course.
✓ I’m meeting my friend tomorrow.

UK: I’m going to ring him later.
US: I’m going to call him later.

Common Mistakes

✗ I will going to call you.
✓ I will call you.

Speaking Tip

Already planned? Use going to or present continuous. Deciding now? Use will.

Mini Vocabulary: tomorrow, next week, soon, later, in a few days
Quick Practice
  1. I think it (will rain / is raining) tomorrow.
  2. She is ______ her friend this evening.
  3. Fix: He going to buy a new car.

Part 2 — Using English More Naturally

Unit 6 — Questions & Question Forms

Questions help us get information, continue conversations, and check understanding. English questions often need an auxiliary verb.

Key Rules

  • Yes/no questions: auxiliary + subject + verb.
  • WH- questions: question word + auxiliary + subject + verb.
  • With the verb be, put be before the subject.

Examples

Do you work here? Where do you live? Are you ready? Why is she late?

UK: Have you got a car?
US: Do you have a car?

Common Mistakes

✗ Where you live?
✓ Where do you live?

Speaking Tip

When unsure, start with do, does, did, is, are, have, or has.

Mini Vocabulary: what, where, when, why, how, which
Quick Practice
  1. Choose: Where you work? / Where do you work?
  2. Complete: ______ do you live?
  3. Fix: Why she is angry?

Unit 7 — Modal Verbs

Modal verbs help us express ability, advice, obligation, permission, and possibility. They are followed by the base verb.

Key Rules

  • can = ability or permission
  • should = advice
  • must = strong obligation
  • have to = external obligation
  • might/may = possibility

Examples

I can swim. You should rest. You must wear a seatbelt. I have to work tomorrow. It might rain later.

UK: You should revise before the test.
US: You should review before the test.

Common Mistakes

✗ He can to swim.
✓ He can swim.

Speaking Tip

Use should for friendly advice. Use must when something is very important.

Mini Vocabulary: take a break, get some rest, ask for help, try again
Quick Practice
  1. You should (go / to go) home.
  2. I ______ swim very well.
  3. Fix: She can to drive.

Unit 8 — Comparatives & Superlatives

Comparatives compare two things. Superlatives compare one thing with a whole group.

Key Rules

  • Short adjectives: add -er / -est.
  • Long adjectives: use more / most.
  • Irregular forms: good → better → best; bad → worse → worst.

Examples

This book is cheaper than that one. She is taller than me. This is the most useful lesson. He is the best person for the job.

Common Mistakes

✗ more better
✓ better

Speaking Tip

Add a bit, much, or far to sound more natural: This is a bit cheaper. This is much better.

Mini Vocabulary: cheap, expensive, useful, comfortable, modern
Quick Practice
  1. This book is (more interesting / interestinger).
  2. He is the ______ person here.
  3. Fix: This is the most cheapest option.

Unit 9 — Adverbs & Adjective Order

Adjectives describe nouns. Adverbs describe verbs, adjectives, or whole sentences. Correct placement makes your English sound natural.

Key Rules

  • Many adverbs end in -ly.
  • Adverbs often go after the verb.
  • Common adjective order: opinion, size, age, colour, noun.

Examples

She speaks clearly. He drives carefully. It is a lovely small café. They bought a big red car.

Common Mistakes

✗ She speaks fluent.
✓ She speaks fluently.

Speaking Tip

If adjective order feels difficult, use one strong adjective instead of many.

Mini Vocabulary: clearly, carefully, quickly, slowly, fluently
Quick Practice
  1. She speaks (fluent / fluently).
  2. Put in order: car / red / big.
  3. Fix: He drives very careful.

Unit 10 — Countable & Uncountable Nouns

Countable nouns can be counted. Uncountable nouns are seen as a whole idea or material, so they do not usually have a plural form.

Key Rules

  • Countable: one apple, two apples.
  • Uncountable: water, advice, information.
  • Use many with countable nouns.
  • Use much with uncountable nouns.
  • Some works with both.

Examples

I have two books. We need some water. She gave me useful advice. There are many people here.

Common Mistakes

✗ informations
✓ information

Speaking Tip

When unsure, use some. It works safely in many everyday sentences.

Mini Vocabulary: advice, information, furniture, luggage, equipment
Quick Practice
  1. Choose: much / many books.
  2. I need some ______.
  3. Fix: She gave me many advice.

Unit 11 — Articles: a, an, the, zero article

Articles show whether we are talking about something general or specific. They are small words, but they are very important in natural English.

Key Rules

  • Use a/an for one general singular countable noun.
  • Use the when the listener knows which thing you mean.
  • Use no article for general plural nouns and many general uncountable nouns.

Examples

I bought a book. The book is on the table. I like coffee. Dogs are friendly animals.

Common Mistakes

✗ I saw dog.
✓ I saw a dog.

Speaking Tip

Ask yourself: Is this specific or general? Specific often needs the.

Mini Vocabulary: book, city, food, music, journey
Quick Practice
  1. I saw (a / the) cat in the garden.
  2. I like ______ coffee.
  3. Fix: She bought book.

Part 3 — Step Into B2

Unit 12 — Present Perfect vs Past Simple

Past simple talks about finished time. Present perfect connects the past with now. This difference is one of the most important grammar points at B1–B2 level.

Key Rules

  • Use past simple when the time is finished or stated.
  • Use present perfect when the time is not important.
  • Use present perfect for life experience and results now.

Examples

I saw that film last night. I have seen that film before. He went to London in 2019. He has been to London several times.

Common Mistakes

✗ I have finished my work last night.
✓ I finished my work last night.

Speaking Tip

If you say when, use past simple.

Mini Vocabulary: yesterday, last week, already, ever, never
Quick Practice
  1. I (saw / have seen) him yesterday.
  2. Have you ever ______ to Italy?
  3. Fix: I have met her in 2020.

Unit 13 — Present Perfect Continuous

We use present perfect continuous for actions that started in the past and continue now, or for recent actions with a visible result.

Key Rules

  • Structure: subject + have/has been + verb-ing.
  • Use for duration: for two hours, since Monday, all day.
  • Use to explain present results: I’m tired because I’ve been working.

Examples

I have been studying all morning. She has been working here since May. They have been waiting for an hour.

Common Mistakes

✗ I have been work.
✓ I have been working.

Speaking Tip

Use this tense when you want to show the activity, not just the result.

Mini Vocabulary: for, since, all day, recently, lately
Quick Practice
  1. I have been ______ all morning.
  2. She has been working here ______ 2022.
  3. Fix: He has been play football.

Unit 14 — Passive Voice

We use passive voice when the action is more important than the person who does it. We also use it when we do not know who did the action.

Key Rules

  • Structure: subject + be + past participle.
  • Use by when you want to say who did the action.
  • The passive is common in formal, academic, and news English.

Examples

English is spoken around the world. The house was built in 1990. The report was written by Anna.

Common Mistakes

✗ The book wrote by him.
✓ The book was written by him.

Speaking Tip

Use passive when the person is unknown, obvious, or not important.

Mini Vocabulary: made, written, built, created, designed
Quick Practice
  1. The book (was write / was written).
  2. English is ______ worldwide.
  3. Fix: The house built in 1990.

Unit 15 — Reported Speech

Reported speech is used when we tell someone what another person said. We often change the tense because we are reporting from a later time.

Key Rules

  • Present often changes to past.
  • Will changes to would.
  • Can changes to could.
  • Use told when you mention the listener: She told me...

Examples

“I am tired.” → She said she was tired. “I will call you.” → He said he would call me. “I can help.” → She said she could help.

Common Mistakes

✗ She said me she was tired.
✓ She told me she was tired.

Speaking Tip

Think of reported speech as taking one step back from the original words.

Mini Vocabulary: said, told, asked, explained, promised
Quick Practice
  1. Change: “I am busy.” → He said...
  2. She said she ______ tired.
  3. Fix: He said he will come.

Unit 16 — Conditionals

Conditionals help us talk about facts, real possibilities, imaginary situations, and past regrets.

Key Rules

  • Zero conditional: If + present, present.
  • First conditional: If + present, will + verb.
  • Second conditional: If + past, would + verb.
  • Third conditional: If + past perfect, would have + past participle.

Examples

If you heat water, it boils. If it rains, I’ll stay home. If I were rich, I would travel. If I had studied, I would have passed.

Common Mistakes

✗ If I will see him, I will tell him.
✓ If I see him, I will tell him.

Speaking Tip

Use “If I were you, I would...” for polite advice.

Mini Vocabulary: if, unless, when, in case, as long as
Quick Practice
  1. If it rains, I (stay / will stay) home.
  2. If I ______ rich, I would travel.
  3. Fix: If I will have time, I will call.

Part 4 — Real Communication

Unit 17 — Relative Clauses

Relative clauses add extra information about a person, thing, place, or time. They help you make longer, more natural sentences.

Key Rules

  • Use who for people.
  • Use which for things.
  • Use where for places.
  • Use that for people or things in many defining clauses.

Examples

This is the teacher who helped me. That is the book that I bought. This is the café where we met. I watched a film which was really interesting.

Common Mistakes

✗ The man which lives next door.
✓ The man who lives next door.

Speaking Tip

Use relative clauses to avoid repeating words: I met a woman. She works here. → I met a woman who works here.

Mini Vocabulary: who, which, that, where, whose
Quick Practice
  1. Choose: The woman (who / which) called you.
  2. This is the restaurant ______ we ate.
  3. Fix: The phone who I bought is expensive.

Unit 18 — Gerunds & Infinitives

Some verbs are followed by -ing forms. Some are followed by to + verb. Some verbs can take both, but the meaning may change.

Key Rules

  • Enjoy, avoid, finish + verb-ing.
  • Want, need, decide + to + verb.
  • After prepositions, use verb-ing.

Examples

I enjoy reading. She decided to leave. He is interested in learning English. We need to talk.

Common Mistakes

✗ I enjoy to read.
✓ I enjoy reading.

Speaking Tip

Learn common verb patterns as phrases: enjoy doing, want to do, good at doing.

Mini Vocabulary: enjoy, avoid, decide, need, plan
Quick Practice
  1. I enjoy (to cook / cooking).
  2. She decided ______ study more.
  3. Fix: He is good at to speak English.

Unit 19 — Phrasal Verbs

Phrasal verbs are verbs with particles like up, on, off, in, and out. They are common in spoken English and often have meanings that are not easy to guess.

Key Rules

  • Some phrasal verbs are separable: turn the light off / turn off the light.
  • Some are inseparable: look after someone.
  • Many phrasal verbs are informal or conversational.

Examples

Please turn off the lights. I grew up in Manchester. She looks after her children. We ran out of milk.

UK: I need to sort out this problem.
US: I need to figure out this problem.

Common Mistakes

✗ I look my little brother after.
✓ I look after my little brother.

Speaking Tip

Do not try to learn huge lists. Learn phrasal verbs in real sentences.

Mini Vocabulary: turn off, look after, run out of, give up, find out
Quick Practice
  1. Complete: Please turn ______ the TV.
  2. I ran ______ milk.
  3. Fix: She looks her sister after.

Unit 20 — Linking Words & Discourse Markers

Linking words connect ideas and make your English sound more organised. They are useful in speaking, writing, presentations, and exams.

Key Rules

  • Use however for contrast.
  • Use because to give a reason.
  • Use therefore to show a result.
  • Use although to contrast two ideas in one sentence.

Examples

I was tired, but I continued working. Although it was raining, we went out. She studied hard; therefore, she passed. I like the job because it is flexible.

Common Mistakes

✗ Although it was late, but we continued.
✓ Although it was late, we continued.

Speaking Tip

Use simple connectors first: and, but, because, so. Then add stronger B2 connectors like however and therefore.

Mini Vocabulary: however, although, therefore, because, meanwhile
Quick Practice
  1. Choose: I stayed home (because / although) I was sick.
  2. Complete: It was expensive. ______, I bought it.
  3. Fix: Although I was tired, but I worked.

Unit 21 — Formal vs Informal English

Formal English is used in professional emails, applications, reports, and polite situations. Informal English is used with friends, family, and casual conversations.

Key Rules

  • Formal English often uses complete forms: I am, cannot, would like.
  • Informal English often uses contractions: I’m, can’t, wanna.
  • Formal writing avoids slang and very casual phrases.

Examples

Informal: Can you send me the info?
Formal: Could you please send me the information?

Informal: Sorry, I can’t make it.
Formal: I apologise, but I am unable to attend.

Common Mistakes

✗ Hey, I want job.
✓ Dear Sir/Madam, I would like to apply for the position.

Speaking Tip

Use “Could you please...?” when you want to sound polite and professional.

Mini Vocabulary: request, attend, provide, assist, apologise
Quick Practice
  1. Make formal: Send me the file.
  2. Choose: I would like / I wanna apply.
  3. Fix: Please give me informations.

Unit 22 — Common Mistakes

This unit reviews common mistakes B1–B2 learners often make. Correcting these mistakes can quickly make your English sound clearer and more natural.

Common Areas

  • Word order
  • Articles
  • Prepositions
  • Present perfect vs past simple
  • Countable and uncountable nouns
  • Modal verbs

Examples

✗ I am agree.
✓ I agree.

✗ She explained me the problem.
✓ She explained the problem to me.

✗ I have 25 years.
✓ I am 25 years old.

✗ I am interested to learn English.
✓ I am interested in learning English.

Speaking Tip

Focus on one mistake at a time. Trying to fix everything at once can slow your speaking down.

Mini Vocabulary: agree, explain, interested, responsible, depend
Quick Practice
  1. Fix: I am agree with you.
  2. Fix: She explained me the rules.
  3. Fix: It depends of the weather.
