English Language Resource

Upgrading Your Vocabulary

Move from basic to natural, expressive English — one upgrade at a time.

A1 / A2 Basic → B1 / B2 Natural

Why This Matters

Using the same basic words makes your English sound flat and repetitive. Small upgrades make a big difference to how natural and confident you sound.

"I was very tired because I had a very busy day. The meeting was very long and very boring."

▸ Every "very" in this sentence can be replaced with one strong, natural word.

1

Intensifiers — Stop Using "Very"

What is an intensifier? It is a word that makes an adjective stronger. In basic English, people use "very" for everything. In natural English, we choose one powerful adjective instead.

The rule: Instead of very + adjective, use one stronger word.

Tip: You can also say "absolutely" or "completely" before the upgraded word for extra emphasis. Example: "absolutely exhausted" or "completely swamped."
✓ Use this when
Telling stories, describing experiences, speaking in meetings, or writing emails. These upgrades work in both formal and informal English.
✗ One rule
Only use an upgrade if you are sure of the meaning. A wrong strong word sounds worse than "very." Learn the word in context first.

Emotions — Express Feelings Naturally

Why upgrade emotion words? Words like "happy," "sad," and "angry" are correct but say very little. A precise emotion word tells people exactly how you felt and shows a much higher level of English.

Adjective vs verb form: Many emotion words have two forms. "I was terrified" = how I felt. "It was terrifying" = what the situation was like. Both are correct — they describe different things.

Nervous vs anxious: Nervous = short term, in the moment (before a presentation). Anxious = a longer-term feeling, sometimes a medical condition. Use carefully.
✓ Use this when
Telling stories about your past, describing how a situation made you feel, giving feedback, or talking about people.
Note on strength
Some emotion words are very strong. "Devastated" is much stronger than "sad." Only use strong words when the feeling is genuinely strong.

Personality & Character — Describing People

When do we describe people? In job interviews, feedback, telling stories, recommending someone, or just talking about your day.

Key tip: Word choice matters — "reserved" sounds neutral and professional, while "shy" sounds more personal. "Unmotivated" is more professional than "lazy." Choose the right register for the context.

Positive vs negative: Be careful — some personality words are clearly negative (cruel, arrogant). Use them carefully when describing real people.
✓ Use this when
Writing a reference, describing a colleague, talking about a character in a book or film, or answering "Tell me about yourself" in an interview.
✗ Avoid when
You are not sure if the word is positive or negative in context. When in doubt, check a dictionary for an example sentence.

Everyday Verbs — GET, GO, MAKE, DO, HAVE

The problem with GET, GO, MAKE, DO: These verbs are correct but very general. In natural, professional English, we use more specific verbs that carry more precise meaning.

Example: "I got an email" is understandable — but "I received an email" is what a B2 speaker says.

Tip: You do not need to replace every basic verb. Focus on written communication (emails, reports) and formal spoken contexts (presentations, interviews) first.
✓ Use this when
Writing emails, giving presentations, job interviews, or any professional context where you want to sound natural and precise.
Note
In casual conversation, "get" and "go" are completely normal. You do not need to upgrade every sentence — only when it matters.
